# Web Discussion / Q&A — February 5, 2026

**Attendees:** Victoria Shaw (Element Three), Kayla Searles (Element Three), Leandra (Kent), Johnnie Arnett (Kent)

**0:00 — Victoria Shaw:** Introduces the call; Kayla Searles joins as Element Three's web/tech lead ("owned our web and tech part of our business") so she can flag pitfalls and client experience patterns.

**2:30 — Johnnie Arnett:** Kent Worldwide is refreshing kentww.com to look and feel more like the new brand book prescribes. Wants more dynamic content, a homepage that opens strong like peer sites. Internal debate about whether the backend (Dynamic Web CMS) is right — other parts of the business prefer WordPress. Dynamic Web is "very secure" but hard to work in. kentww.com and grainprocessing.com (GPC) both run on Dynamic Web. Kentww.com is "a dumb website" — not transactional, meant to be "a beautiful parent company website," and it isn't hitting that bar.

**4:02–4:39 — Victoria/Johnnie:** Inspiration cited: Mars, Clorox (parent-company sites, not direct competitors). Kent's 100-year anniversary is 2027.

**4:52–6:34 — Johnnie:** For 2027, wants more robust history/heritage content. Company is writing a coffee-table book on Kent's history and wants the website to mirror its look, feel, and table of contents. Cites the White House's "Founders Museum" (America's 250th) as a model — tiles for individual stories, click into video + article. Example story tiles: invention of Maltrin (GPC), invention of World's Best Cat Litter, founding of Kent via Kent 34 Baby Beef (Kent Nutrition).

**6:34–7:44 — Victoria/Johnnie:** Undecided whether a platform switch would be consistent across Kent, GPC, and other subs, or vary by business unit. Internal alignment on this hasn't happened yet — depends who you ask. IT has the platform opinion but isn't focused on design or content-update speed.

**8:26–9:03 — Johnnie:** Kent's intranet (internal comms platform) is far easier to update and make beautiful than the external website — which is "an issue" and "frustrating" given the intranet has a much smaller/less important audience.

**8:38–12:34 — Kayla Searles:** Compares CMS options generally. WordPress is the most popular CMS worldwide — very flexible, but security depends more on hosting/management than the platform itself. Scope depends on ambition: single site (Kent Worldwide) vs. multi-site (Kent + GPC + subsidiaries). Multi-site/shared-component CMS setups (WordPress multi-site or similar) let you build once and share across sites, but changes to shared components can lock in old constraints across every site on it — cites a past client with 5–6 subsidiary sites sharing components that became a redesign bottleneck.

**12:34–13:54 — Johnnie:** Explicitly scopes concern to kentww.com (most concerned) and grainprocessing.com (somewhat concerned) — no other subsidiary sites are in scope. Kent Feeds, World's Best Cat Litter, Mrs. Wages, etc. are brands under the Kent Worldwide umbrella, not separate site properties to redesign. Wants the site to look good by January 1, 2027 and is explicit about avoiding scope creep.

**13:54–14:15 — Kayla:** Given the ask (style refresh + history/background content), that's achievable within a year without a full replatform.

**14:16–15:33 — Johnnie:** No in-house developers. Johnnie and Leandra are "the most dangerous" people in Dynamic Web internally, doing small changes/news posts themselves. IT doesn't build; a third-party dev partner (Lithia, later replaced by Codal) does the actual development work.

**15:33–18:18 — Johnnie/Leandra:** IT's role is largely gatekeeping/ticketing, not building. Lithia is the third-party dev partner Kent relies on for anything requiring development. Mission-critical: get IT bought into whichever direction is chosen. Decision-making is a group/committee process, not a single owner — described later as "brand center of excellence" work.

**18:26–21:02 — Victoria/Kayla:** General industry watch-outs for platform/redesign decisions: understand appetite for replatforming vs. restyling, and whether restyling means reskinning existing components or introducing new module types (which may require content adjustments). There's a hybrid path between "leave it alone" and "full rebuild."

**21:16–21:41 — Kayla/Leandra:** Clarifies the relationship with Lithia — they are developers Kent works with for custom/out-of-the-box builds, not a marketing agency.

**21:45–24:08 — Johnnie (screen share of kentww.com homepage):** Walks through current site pain points: a persistent visual glitch/line on the homepage; outdated photography; frustration that requesting new photos led to "let's explore a new backend instead." Likes Mars.com's homepage carousel + rotating news module that keeps the homepage feeling fresh (brand-forward, not just corporate). Wants Kent Worldwide content mix to be roughly 50% corporate news / 50% brand-specific news, updated at least monthly.

**25:03–26:31 — Kayla/Johnnie/Leandra:** No real integration between kentww.com, grainprocessing.com, and Frostline/Dole (also on Dynamic Web) beyond being on the same CMS — a shared-component change would need Lithia to implement it across all three sites, which Leandra explicitly says she wouldn't call "integrated."

**26:44–27:10 — Johnnie:** Wants a "Brands" dropdown under the main nav showing major categories (Pet Care, Animal Nutrition, Food Service, Custom Ingredients, Hydration) rather than just individual brand names — this is out-of-the-box functionality on Dynamic Web that was simply never turned on. Notes that Kent does meaningful custom/co-man food manufacturing work that isn't represented anywhere on kentww.com today.

**27:10–28:58 — Victoria/Johnnie:** Discussion of engagement model — E3 could be a "thinking partner" guiding developers via a web style guide / digital brand vision (translator/product-manager role), or Kent could own the developer relationship directly and loop E3 in only at milestones. Johnnie leans toward "great design direction + a good graphic designer working from the brand book is probably enough," and explicitly doesn't want a heavy digital style guide with granular decisions like H3 vs. H4 usage — doesn't see the value internally.

**30:08–34:06 — Kayla:** Explains E3's typical process: translate an existing brand style guide (or newly created brand work) into a *web* style guide — color palette, fonts, image look/feel, iconography — as it should appear on the site. Built in Figma (cloud-based, InDesign-like, multiplayer). Client sees style guide + a handful of applied modules together before full-scale design, since color/type in isolation doesn't land the same as applied examples. Heading hierarchy (H1–H6) is defined per template/module during build to protect accessibility and page hierarchy — content managers get limited, structured choices rather than open control, since multi-editor sites tend to erode heading hygiene over time.

**34:06–34:53 — Victoria/Johnnie:** Budget for a website redesign was anticipated in fiscal planning, but not for a full platform shift (which would likely be an IT budget line, not Johnnie's).

**34:53–37:15 — Johnnie/Kayla/Victoria:** Reiterates the current hero image blocks news visibility; storytelling opportunities are constrained by how hard it is to post news. No formal customer/visitor feedback loop on kentww.com, though Google Analytics shows real traffic. Mobile: some sections are fine, but the News section (and Careers) has inconsistent/odd image cropping across templates — a likely module-level fix in a redesign.

**37:15–38:07 — Kayla:** Curious whether the Lithia relationship is hourly or retainer — Johnnie isn't sure; IT manages/pays for it, likely a mix, and IT does ask around budget time what's wanted for the website in the coming year so it can be planned for (vs. billed hourly if unplanned).

**38:36–39:35 — Johnnie/Leandra:** Clarifies the "Our Brands" nav vision: category tab (e.g., Pet Care) → its own landing page with a small logo board of brands in that category → clicking a brand exits kentww.com to that brand's own site (e.g., Antec) — Kent Worldwide is a "pushing you along" layer, not a destination for brand content itself.

**39:35–43:15 — Leandra/Kayla:** Given the January 1, 2027 hard deadline for Kent Worldwide, replatforming is *reasonable* to consider for kentww.com specifically. GPC is a different story — its more e-commerce/cart-like experience means replatforming would require more content rethinking and coordination, adding real risk to the timeline; a pure Dynamic Web restyle is comparatively low-risk there. Recommended next step regardless of platform decision: build a thematic roadmap — get a web style guide made, audit/restyle existing components, and identify opportunities for net-new components — before tackling harder structural pieces like navigation (which should wait until new content/IA is ready to go live simultaneously).

**43:18–44:39 — Victoria/Kayla:** Tangent: Kayla explains headless CMS (e.g., Sanity, used for a past client, Bex Hybrids) — separates the back-end "database" from front-end "styles," useful when a client has massive content libraries that need to be referenced across multiple platforms/apps. For Kent Worldwide's scale, a headless CMS is likely overkill/cost-prohibitive unless they plan to create content at that volume.

**44:39–46:53 — Johnnie/Kayla:** Direct question: "What would you do if you were us?" Kayla's honest take: Dynamic Web is clunky/awkward to use, but it's unclear how much of that is the platform vs. how it was originally built/developed (possibly a Lithia execution issue, not a Dynamic Web limitation). Recommends putting a plan together, testing what's actually achievable in Dynamic Web via Lithia before committing either way — the risk to avoid is getting Kent excited about a plan only to have developers say the CMS can't support it. If Kent were comfortable with WordPress, moving to it wouldn't be a heavy lift for a site of this scope.
